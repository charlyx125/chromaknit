# Decision 002.1: Cluster Count — 5 to 10

**Date:** April 2026
**Status:** Accepted and Implemented
**Author:** Joyce Chong
**Parent ADR:** [002 — Recoloring Strategy](002-recoloring-strategy.md)

---

## Context

After deploying with 5 K-means clusters (the default since Phase 1), the recolored garments looked flat and bland. My first instinct was that the recoloring algorithm was wrong — but that didn't make sense. The algorithm maps dark yarn colours to dark garment pixels and light yarn colours to light garment pixels (ADR 002). If the mapping logic is correct, the only way the output looks flat is if the **input** colours aren't spread wide enough.

I checked the extracted palettes and confirmed: 5 clusters produced 5 mid-range colours that all looked nearly identical. A mint yarn gave 5 similar greens — no dark undertone for the shadows, no bright highlight for the raised stitches. The darkest extracted colour wasn't dark enough. The recoloring algorithm was doing its job correctly — it was mapping dark-to-dark — but the "dark" it had to work with was barely darker than the "light."

Looking at the recolored garment closely, parts of the knit texture — the loops, the folds between stitches — weren't showing through the way they do in a real photo. The texture was there in the original garment image, but the recoloring was flattening it. With only 5 clusters, a typical extraction might give 2 light shades, 2 mid-bright shades, and 1 dark — not a smooth gradient from shadow to highlight, but a few clumps in the middle with weak extremes.

The problem wasn't the recoloring. It was the colour range. The palette had breadth but no depth. To preserve knit texture, you need a gradient — enough colour steps that the darkest shadow in a stitch fold maps to something genuinely dark, and the brightest highlight on a raised loop maps to something genuinely bright.

---

## Hypothesis

Increasing from 5 to 10 clusters would force K-means to differentiate between the main colour and its shadow/highlight variants. These extra clusters would naturally map to the garment's dark (fold/shadow) and bright (highlight) pixels via the existing brightness-based colour mapping, producing more realistic texture without any algorithm changes.

---

## Extraction Comparison

### Mint Yarn

5 clusters produced all similar mid-range greens — no dark shadow tone, no light highlight. 10 clusters picks up darker teal/shadow tones and lighter highlights, giving the palette both **breadth** (range of hues) and **depth** (range of brightness).

### Soft Purple Yarn

5 clusters produced all mid-range purples with very similar values. 10 clusters catches dark shadow purple and light lavender highlights that 5 collapsed into mid-tones.

## Recoloring Result Comparison

### Soft Purple on Baby Knit Socks

- **10 clusters:** Deeper shadows between stitches, more defined texture, individual loops and folds visible
- **5 clusters:** Flatter, more uniform purple, less shadow definition — like someone turned the contrast down

→ See `docs/comparisons/5-vs-10-clusters-purple-socks.png`

### Mint on Baby Knit Socks

- **10 clusters:** Richer shadows in the ankle folds, more visible stitch definition on the foot, darker sole edge giving dimension. Colour reads more true to mint.
- **5 clusters:** Flatter, more washed out, less shadow depth

## Why Not DBSCAN?

Considered switching to DBSCAN (density-based clustering) since the goal was to capture sparse shadow pixels. However, DBSCAN's density-based approach would likely label those sparse shadow pixels as **noise** — the exact opposite of what we want. Sparse dark pixels scattered across the yarn image don't form dense clusters.

K-means at 10 solves this naturally: it's forced to find exactly 10 centroids, so it *has* to differentiate between the main blue and the dark shadow blue. The extraction percentages tell the recoloring pipeline which colours are dominant (30%) vs shadow tones (3%), and those percentages feed directly into the weighted brightness mapping.

The ADR 001 analysis still holds: "We need exactly K colours. DBSCAN solves a different problem (unknown cluster count)." With 10, we're giving K-means more room to find the nuance.

---

## Decision

**Changed default from 5 to 10 clusters.** The improvement is consistent across both light (mint) and dark (purple) yarns. The cost is negligible — MiniBatchKMeans at 10 clusters is barely slower than 5.

---

## UI Impact

With 10 clusters, showing all 10 as full colour cards would be visually noisy. The solution:

- **Tab 1 (pick yarn):** Full colour palette cards shown if user navigates back — useful for demo/interview
- **Tab 2 (upload garment):** Colours displayed as small inline dots (18px circles) — compact enough for 10 without visual noise. During recoloring, dots animate with a bouncing wave effect

---

## Trade-offs

| Aspect | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Extraction speed | Negligible — MiniBatchKMeans at 10 barely slower than 5 |
| Colour accuracy | Better — captures shadow and highlight tones |
| UI complexity | Mitigated — dots instead of cards on garment tab |
| Noise risk | Some clusters may capture lighting artifacts, not real colours — acceptable, they still contribute to realistic recoloring |
